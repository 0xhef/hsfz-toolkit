## Nominative fair use, three-factor test

Under US trademark law (*New Kids on the Block v. News America
Publishing Inc.*, 971 F.2d 302 (9th Cir. 1992); codified in the
majority of other US circuits), a third party may use another's
trademark to refer to that other's product without infringement
when:

1. **Necessity.** The product or service in question is not readily
   identifiable without use of the mark. This project implements a
   security research toolkit for the HSFZ diagnostic tunnel (a
   protocol developed for use on vehicles manufactured by BMW AG)
   and targets Bosch MEVD17-class engine control units. Descriptive
   identification of these protocols and hardware is not practicable
   without naming them.
2. **Minimum use.** Only so much of the mark is used as is
   reasonably necessary to identify the product. This project uses
   the plain word mark "BMW" (no logo, no roundel, no stylized
   wordmark, no "Ultimate Driving Machine" slogan, no BMW M
   sub-brand, no BMW Individual sub-brand, no BMW trade dress).
   Analogous minimal use applies to references to "MEVD17",
   "Motronic", and "ISTA".
3. **No suggestion of sponsorship or endorsement.** This project
   uses an independent product name (*BMSecResearch*), carries an
   unambiguous disclaimer of affiliation in the application itself
   (header and footer), in the repository README, in the application
   metadata (`tauri.conf.json` longDescription), and in this file.

Parallel "referential use" protection exists under European
trademark law in EU Trademark Regulation Article 14(1)(c), which
permits use of a third-party trademark "for the purpose of
identifying or referring to goods or services as those of the
proprietor of that trade mark, in particular where the use of the
trade mark is necessary to indicate the intended purpose of a
product or service, in particular as accessories or spare parts."
